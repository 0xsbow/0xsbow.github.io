Complete VAPT Methodology Guide: End-to-End Framework

Published: Dec 2025

Vulnerability Assessment and Penetration Testing (VAPT) is a comprehensive security evaluation process that identifies, exploits, and documents security weaknesses in organizational systems. This guide provides a complete framework covering all five phases of penetration testing, industry-standard methodologies, foundational security triads, and practical implementation strategies.

Understanding VAPT: Core Concepts

Vulnerability Assessment vs. Penetration Testing

Vulnerability Assessment: A systematic process of identifying, classifying, and prioritizing vulnerabilities in systems, networks, and applications. It focuses on DISCOVERY without attempting to exploit vulnerabilities.

Penetration Testing: An authorized, simulated attack on systems to evaluate security posture by attempting to exploit identified vulnerabilities. It demonstrates IMPACT and real-world attack chains.

üí° Key Difference

Vulnerability Assessment identifies "What's broken?" while Penetration Testing answers "How can we break in?" VAPT combines both approaches for comprehensive security evaluation.

The Security Fundamentals: Two Critical Triads

1. CIA Triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability)

The CIA triad represents the three pillars of information security that VAPT activities protect:

üîí Confidentiality

Ensuring sensitive information is accessible only to authorized individuals. Tests focus on authentication, authorization, and encryption mechanisms.

‚úÖ Integrity

Ensuring data accuracy and completeness throughout its lifecycle. Tests evaluate input validation, access controls, and change management.

‚ö° Availability

Ensuring systems and data are accessible when needed. Tests include DoS/DDoS testing, backup verification, and failover mechanisms.

2. People, Process, and Technology Triad (PPT)

Successful VAPT engagement requires balancing human, procedural, and technical factors:

üë• People

Security awareness, training, access controls, insider threat programs, and human error prevention. VAPT tests social engineering, phishing, and credential handling.

‚öôÔ∏è Process

Policies, procedures, incident response, patch management, and security governance. VAPT evaluates process adherence and control effectiveness.

üñ•Ô∏è Technology

Systems, applications, networks, and infrastructure. VAPT identifies technical vulnerabilities across all technology layers.

The Cyber Kill Chain: Attack Methodology Framework

The Cyber Kill Chain, developed by Lockheed Martin, provides a structured model of how attackers operate. VAPT simulates each phase:

  1. Reconnaissance: Gathering intelligence about target systems (passive and active)
  2. Weaponization: Creating or modifying tools and payloads for exploitation
  3. Delivery: Transmitting weaponized payloads through attack vectors (email, web, physical)
  4. Exploitation: Executing attacks to trigger malicious actions on target systems
  5. Installation: Installing backdoors, malware, or persistence mechanisms
  6. Command & Control: Establishing communication with compromised systems
  7. Actions on Objectives: Executing attacker goals (exfiltration, destruction, lateral movement)

‚úì VAPT's Role in Kill Chain Defense

VAPT activities focus on detecting and interrupting each phase of the kill chain, providing recommendations for defensive improvements at each stage.

The Five Phases of Penetration Testing

Phase 1: Pre-Engagement and Planning

1 Planning & Scoping

Before any testing begins, proper planning ensures legal compliance, clear objectives, and manageable scope.

Key Activities:

  • Scope Definition: Define in-scope and out-of-scope systems, IP ranges, and applications
  • Rules of Engagement (RoE): Document authorized testing windows, contact information, and restrictions
  • Legal Documentation: Obtain written authorization, signed NDA, and liability waivers
  • Objectives Definition: Establish testing goals (compliance, vulnerability discovery, security posture assessment)
  • Resource Allocation: Determine team composition, timeline, and budget
  • Risk Assessment: Identify potential risks of testing activities and mitigation strategies

Deliverables:

‚úì Statement of Work (SoW)
‚úì Rules of Engagement Document
‚úì Signed Authorization Letter
‚úì Testing Scope Document
‚úì Timeline and Resource Plan
‚úì Risk Assessment Report

Phase 2: Reconnaissance (Information Gathering)

2 Reconnaissance

Comprehensive information gathering about target systems using both passive and active techniques.

Passive Reconnaissance (No Direct Contact):

  • OSINT (Open Source Intelligence): Public records, social media, company websites
  • DNS Enumeration: DNS records, subdomains, mail servers (nslookup, dig, whois)
  • Search Engine Dorking: Google, Bing, Shodan, Censys queries for exposed data
  • Website Analysis: Technologies, metadata, comments, JavaScript analysis
  • Social Engineering Preparation: Email harvesting, LinkedIn profiling, employee research

Active Reconnaissance (Direct Interaction):

  • Port Scanning: Identify open ports and services (nmap, masscan)
  • Service Enumeration: Identify service versions, banners, and configurations
  • Network Mapping: Identify network topology and interconnections
  • Vulnerability Scanning: Automated scanning for known vulnerabilities (Nessus, OpenVAS)
  • Web Application Mapping: Identify endpoints, parameters, and functionality (Burp Suite, OWASP ZAP)

Tools Used:

Passive: whois, nslookup, dig, host, Shodan, Censys, OSINT tools
Active: nmap, masscan, Nessus, OpenVAS, Burp Suite, OWASP ZAP, gobuster

Phase 3: Vulnerability Assessment and Analysis

3 Vulnerability Assessment

Systematic identification of security weaknesses and detailed analysis of each discovered vulnerability.

Assessment Activities:

  • Manual Vulnerability Testing: Hands-on testing against identified attack surfaces
  • Configuration Review: Evaluate security configurations (TLS versions, cipher suites, headers)
  • Access Control Testing: Identify privilege escalation and lateral movement opportunities
  • Application Testing: Input validation, authentication bypass, business logic flaws (OWASP Top 10)
  • Cryptography Review: Weak encryption, insecure key management, hashing algorithms
  • Patch Management Evaluation: Assess missing security patches and update cadence

Phase 4: Exploitation and Testing

4 Exploitation

Authorized, controlled attempts to exploit identified vulnerabilities to demonstrate real-world impact.

Exploitation Strategies:

  • Direct Exploitation: Exploit individual vulnerabilities with available PoC code
  • Chaining Vulnerabilities: Combine multiple vulnerabilities to increase impact
  • Privilege Escalation: Escalate from low-privilege user to system administrator
  • Lateral Movement: Move across network to access additional systems
  • Persistence Techniques: Establish backdoors and persistent access mechanisms
  • Data Exfiltration: Demonstrate ability to access and extract sensitive data

Attack Simulation Scenarios:

Scenario 1: External Attacker
Goal: Gain initial foothold via internet-exposed systems

Scenario 2: Insider Threat
Goal: Escalate from standard user to administrator

Scenario 3: Lateral Movement
Goal: Move from DMZ to internal network

Scenario 4: Data Breach
Goal: Locate and exfiltrate sensitive data

Exploitation Tools:

Metasploit Framework, Burp Suite, SQLMap, hashcat, John the Ripper,
Impacket tools, Empire, Mimikatz, BloodHound, PowerShell Empire

‚ö†Ô∏è Exploitation Guidelines

Always obtain explicit written permission. Limit exploitation scope. Avoid data destruction. Never disrupt production systems. Maintain detailed logs of exploitation activities. Have rollback plans ready.

Phase 5: Reporting and Remediation

5 Reporting & Remediation

Comprehensive documentation of findings and actionable recommendations for remediation.

Report Components:

  • Executive Summary: High-level overview for C-level executives (1-2 pages)
  • Risk Rating: Critical, High, Medium, Low based on CVSS scores
  • Vulnerability Details: Each finding with description, impact, and proof of concept
  • Remediation Recommendations: Specific, actionable steps to fix each issue
  • Timeline: Suggested patching and remediation schedule
  • Testing Methodology: Detailed explanation of testing approach
  • Tools and Techniques Used: Documentation for reproducibility

CVSS v3.1 Scoring Example:

CVSS:3.1/AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N/S:U/C:H/I:H/A:H = 9.8 (Critical)

AV (Attack Vector): Network/Adjacent/Local/Physical
AC (Attack Complexity): Low/High
PR (Privileges Required): None/Low/High
UI (User Interaction): None/Required
S (Scope): Unchanged/Changed
C (Confidentiality): None/Low/High
I (Integrity): None/Low/High
A (Availability): None/Low/High

Remediation Prioritization:

Priority 1 (Critical): CVSS 9.0-10.0 ‚Üí Fix within 24-48 hours
Priority 2 (High): CVSS 7.0-8.9 ‚Üí Fix within 1 week
Priority 3 (Medium): CVSS 4.0-6.9 ‚Üí Fix within 2-4 weeks
Priority 4 (Low): CVSS 0.1-3.9 ‚Üí Fix within 30 days

  • Test for business logic data validation
  • Test for ability to forge requests

  • Test number of times a resource can be accessed
  • Test for circumventing workflow

  • Test for SQL injection
  • Test for command injection
  • Test for LDAP injection
  • Test for XML injection
  • Test for XPath injection
  • Test for cross-site scripting (XSS)
  • Test for cross-site request forgery (CSRF)
  • Test for buffer overflow
  • Test for malicious file upload
  • Test for path traversal

Error Handling

  • Test for improper error handling
  • Test for stack traces
  • Test for sensitive information in error messages

  • Test for weak SSL/TLS configuration
  • Test for cryptographic algorithms weakness

Comprehensive Test Cases and Scenarios

Authentication Test Cases

TC-AUTH-001: Test for password policy enforcement
  Input: Common passwords (123456, password, admin)
  Expected: Rejection of weak passwords

TC-AUTH-002: Test for account lockout mechanism
  Input: 5+ failed login attempts
  Expected: Account locked for 30 minutes

TC-AUTH-003: Test for brute force protection
  Input: Rapid login attempts from same IP
  Expected: Rate limiting or CAPTCHA triggered

TC-AUTH-004: Test for credential storage
  Verify: Passwords hashed with bcrypt/scrypt/argon2
  Expected: No plaintext passwords in database

TC-AUTH-005: Test for session fixation
  Action: Obtain session ID, login, verify session changes
  Expected: New session ID after login

Authorization Test Cases

TC-AUTHZ-001: Test for horizontal privilege escalation
  Action: User A attempts to access User B's data
  Expected: Access denied

TC-AUTHZ-002: Test for vertical privilege escalation
  Action: Standard user attempts admin functions
  Expected: Insufficient privileges error

TC-AUTHZ-003: Test for insecure direct object references (IDOR)
  Input: Modify resource ID in URL (user/1 ‚Üí user/2)
  Expected: Access only own resources

TC-AUTHZ-004: Test for missing access controls
  Action: Unauthenticated user accesses protected endpoints
  Expected: Redirect to login

Data Protection Test Cases

TC-DATA-001: Test for data encryption in transit
  Verification: All HTTPS connections
  Expected: TLS 1.2+, no SSL/early TLS versions

TC-DATA-002: Test for data encryption at rest
  Verification: Check database encryption
  Expected: AES-256 or equivalent

TC-DATA-003: Test for PII exposure in logs
  Analysis: Review application logs
  Expected: No passwords, SSN, credit cards in logs

TC-DATA-004: Test for data retention policies
  Verification: Backup and deletion procedures
  Expected: Data deleted per retention policy

Business Logic Test Cases

TC-BL-001: Test for race conditions in transactions
  Action: Simultaneous requests for same resource
  Expected: Proper locking/sequencing

TC-BL-002: Test for workflow bypass
  Action: Skip steps in multi-step process
  Expected: Cannot proceed without all steps

TC-BL-003: Test for price manipulation
  Input: Modify item price in shopping cart
  Expected: Price from server used, not client

TC-BL-004: Test for duplicate transaction prevention
  Action: Submit same request twice
  Expected: Only one transaction processed

People, Process, and Technology Assessment Framework

People Assessment

  • Social Engineering Testing: Phishing campaigns, pretexting, physical security bypasses
  • Security Awareness: Employee knowledge of security policies and procedures
  • Insider Threat Simulation: Compromised employee account scenarios
  • Access Management Review: Segregation of duties, least privilege verification
  • Credential Handling: Password sharing, sticky notes, email transmission of credentials

Process Assessment

  • Change Management Review: Are security controls updated with changes?
  • Incident Response Verification: Test incident response playbooks and team capabilities
  • Patch Management Validation: Identify missing security patches and update frequency
  • Access Control Procedures: Onboarding/offboarding processes, access request approvals
  • Security Governance: Policy documentation, compliance verification

Technology Assessment

  • Infrastructure Security: Network segmentation, firewall rules, IDS/IPS effectiveness
  • Application Security: Code vulnerabilities, frameworks, dependencies
  • Endpoint Security: Antivirus, EDR, encryption, patch status
  • Cloud Security: Configuration, access controls, data protection
  • Logging and Monitoring: Log collection, retention, alerting capabilities

Post-Engagement Activities

Debrief Meeting

Schedule meeting with stakeholders to discuss:

  • Summary of findings and overall risk posture
  • Critical issues requiring immediate attention
  • Questions and clarifications on test methodology
  • Prioritization of remediation efforts

Remediation Support

  • Provide additional technical guidance on fixes
  • Assist with proof-of-concept validation
  • Support re-testing after remediation
  • Offer ongoing security recommendations

Re-testing and Verification

  • Verify critical findings have been remediated
  • Confirm no regressions introduced by fixes
  • Validate new controls are effective
  • Document remediation timeline and completion

Key Takeaways and Best Practices

  • Holistic Approach: VAPT must address people, processes, and technology comprehensively
  • Risk-Based Prioritization: Focus on high-impact vulnerabilities aligned with business criticality
  • Continuous Improvement: Regular testing cycles (annual minimum) ensure sustained security posture
  • Methodology Standardization: Follow established frameworks (OWASP, NIST, PTES) for consistency and completeness
  • Threat-Informed Testing: Align tests with actual threat actors (MITRE ATT&CK)
  • Clear Communication: Executive summaries and actionable recommendations drive remediation
  • Automation and Tooling: Leverage scanners for initial identification, supplement with manual testing
  • Documentation: Maintain detailed records for compliance, trend analysis, and improvement tracking

Conclusion

VAPT is a critical component of modern cybersecurity programs. By following the five-phase methodology, aligning with industry-standard frameworks (OWASP, NIST, MITRE ATT&CK, ISO/IEC 27001), and maintaining a balanced focus on people, processes, and technology, organizations can effectively identify and remediate security vulnerabilities before attackers exploit them.

The CIA triad and PPT triad provide conceptual foundations, while the cyber kill chain helps simulate realistic attack scenarios. Comprehensive test cases ensure nothing is missed, and detailed reporting drives effective remediation. When executed properly, VAPT becomes a strategic investment in organizational security and resilience.

Remember: The goal of VAPT is not to find the most vulnerabilities, but to find the vulnerabilities that matter most to your business and help your organization reduce risk.
